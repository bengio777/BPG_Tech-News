## Month in Review

February 2026 will be remembered as the month AI governance became a contact sport. Five stories defined the arc: an Anthropic valuation that stunned Wall Street, a Pentagon confrontation that exposed the fault lines between AI safety and national security, OpenAI's historic $110 billion raise that reset every prior notion of private market scale, a new category of AI-assisted cyberattack that arrived without warning, and an AI system that autonomously discovered and patched all twelve vulnerabilities in one of the internet's most critical cryptographic libraries. Taken together, February wasn't a month of incremental progress — it was the month several multi-year trajectories reached their logical conclusions simultaneously.

**1. Anthropic's $30B Series G — The Valuation That Anchored the Month**

On February 21, Anthropic's $30 billion Series G — led by GIC and Coatue — officially registered as the second-largest venture deal in history, valuing the company at $380 billion. That number doubled its September 2025 valuation in five months and arrived on the back of $14 billion in annualized revenue growing 10x annually for three consecutive years. Eight Fortune 10 companies are paying Claude customers. The raise set the tone for everything that followed: a market that has decided frontier AI companies are winner-take-most bets, and that capital concentration is the appropriate response.

**2. The Anthropic-Pentagon Standoff (and Its Aftermath)**

The month's most dramatic arc ran from February 23 to February 28. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth summoned Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei to the Pentagon and threatened a "supply-chain risk" designation unless Anthropic loosened its acceptable-use-policy restrictions barring Claude from fully autonomous weapons systems and domestic mass surveillance. Anthropic's response — a revised Responsible Scaling Policy v3.0 published February 24 that removed the hard cap on training more capable models — was widely read as a capitulation to Pentagon pressure, though the company maintained that unconditional safety mitigations remained in place. Meanwhile, xAI signed the Pentagon's "all lawful use" contract that Anthropic had refused, deploying Grok in classified intelligence and weapons development systems.

The denouement arrived February 27–28: Trump designated Anthropic a "Supply-Chain Risk to National Security," ordering federal agencies to cease procurement. Within hours, OpenAI announced a competing Pentagon deal — but with DoD explicitly accepting OpenAI's policy prohibitions on autonomous weapons and domestic mass surveillance. The same restrictions that got Anthropic blacklisted were accepted without incident for OpenAI. The asymmetry revealed that the confrontation was never purely about policy terms.

**3. OpenAI's $110B Round — The Largest Private Raise in History**

On February 27, OpenAI closed $50 billion from Amazon, $30 billion from Nvidia, and $30 billion from SoftBank at a $730 billion pre-money valuation. Amazon's commitment carries an expanded AWS partnership worth $100 billion in compute over eight years; Nvidia's investment includes GPU infrastructure agreements. The round remains open. Three months earlier, analysts were flagging OpenAI's $207 billion projected cumulative cash gap through 2030. The February raise doesn't erase that gap, but it funds the runway to reach the revenue inflection where infrastructure spending becomes self-sustaining — or to reach an IPO first.

**4. AI-Assisted Cyberattacks Arrive at Scale**

Amazon Threat Intelligence documented a Russian-speaking, financially motivated threat actor who used a system called ARXON to query DeepSeek and Claude for structured attack playbooks, lateral movement plans, and credential staging strategies. The operator — described as relatively low-skill — compromised over 600 FortiGate appliances across 55 countries in five weeks by targeting exposed management ports and weak single-factor credentials. No FortiGate zero-day was required. The attack chain previously required teams of skilled operators to execute manually. February 2026 is the month that AI-accelerated exploitation moved from proof-of-concept to documented operational reality.

IBM's 2026 X-Force Threat Intelligence Index, published February 25, provided the aggregate picture: vulnerability exploitation is now the leading attack vector at 40% of incidents, driven by AI tools that accelerate identification; active ransomware and extortion groups surged 49% year-over-year; North America became the most attacked region for the first time in six years.

**5. AISLE Completes the Full Vulnerability Disclosure Cycle for OpenSSL**

The January 27 OpenSSL security release contained twelve zero-day vulnerabilities — all originally discovered by an AI system called AISLE. Three of the bugs had been present since 1998–2000, surviving decades of human analysis and automated scanning. AISLE authored patches for five of the twelve that were accepted directly into the official release. This is the first documented case of an AI system completing the full disclosure cycle — discovery through patch authorship — for a cryptographic library of OpenSSL's criticality and deployment scale. It is a preview of what AI-accelerated security research looks like when it works.

**6. Anthropic Accuses Chinese AI Labs of Industrial-Scale Model Distillation**

Anthropic publicly accused DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax of running coordinated campaigns to extract Claude's capabilities through model distillation — generating over 16 million exchanges via approximately 24,000 fraudulently created accounts routed through commercial proxy services. The practice, Anthropic warned, produces models without safety guardrails and could enable offensive cyber operations, disinformation campaigns, and mass surveillance. The accusation landed the same week that OpenAI reported Chinese law enforcement had used ChatGPT to design an influence operation targeting Japan's Prime Minister — stopped by ChatGPT's refusal, then continued using locally-hosted Chinese models.

**7. Cisco SD-WAN Zero-Day — Exploited Since 2023, CVSS 10.0**

CISA issued Emergency Directive 26-03 on February 25 for CVE-2026-20127, an authentication bypass in Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Controller and Manager (CVSS 10.0) that allows unauthenticated remote attackers to obtain administrative privileges. Threat actor UAT-8616 has exploited it since at least 2023, chaining it with CVE-2022-20775 for root escalation. Federal agencies were required to patch by 5:00 PM ET on February 27. A three-year exploitation window before public disclosure is a systemic failure of the vulnerability disclosure process.

**8. Meta's $60B AMD Chip Deal — Challenging Nvidia's Compute Monopoly**

Meta committed to purchasing up to $60 billion in AMD AI chips and CPUs over five years, with AMD granting Meta warrants to acquire up to 160 million AMD shares at $0.01 each — roughly 10% of the company. AMD shares jumped over 10% on the announcement. The deal is a deliberate diversification away from Nvidia dependency among hyperscalers, and represents the first credible structural challenge to Nvidia's compute monopoly at enterprise scale.

**9. IDMerit KYC Breach — Approximately 1 Billion Records**

On February 19, IDMerit's Know Your Customer identity verification platform suffered a breach exposing approximately one billion records including names, government ID details, and biometric data. The downstream exposure extends to every organization that relied on IDMerit for customer verification — a cascading identity risk that identity-centric security programs were not designed to handle at this scale.

**10. Scattered Lapsus ShinyHunters — A New Threat Alliance at Scale**

The fusion of Scattered Spider, Lapsus$, and ShinyHunters into a coordinated alliance claimed over 60 million breached records in early 2026, with escalation tactics that include executive swatting, DDoS, and proactive regulator and media notification. The January wave originated from social engineering attacks impersonating IT staff. February saw the group publish 12.4 million CarGurus customer records. The formation of a capable, well-resourced extortion alliance with diversified escalation tools represents a maturation of the threat actor ecosystem that enterprise security teams have not fully priced into their incident response assumptions.

## Trend Lines

**AI Safety vs. National Security Becomes a Live Conflict**

The Anthropic-Pentagon arc — which compressed a warning, a policy reversal, a competitor contract, and a federal blacklisting into five days — revealed a structural tension that will not resolve easily. Anthropic's original RSP hard caps were designed to prevent capability races; the Pentagon's objection was that those caps precluded the use cases the military actually wants. That OpenAI secured a comparable contract while maintaining comparable policy restrictions suggests the conflict was partly political, but the underlying tension is real and will recur as AI systems approach capabilities the DoD wants to use in contexts that safety-focused labs have explicitly prohibited. Expect the governance battle to shift from voluntary policy commitments to enforceable contract terms and congressional action in Q2.

**Capital Concentration Is Selecting Winners Pre-IPO**

February's funding announcements — $30 billion for Anthropic, $110 billion for OpenAI, $16 billion for Waymo, $1.2 billion for Wayve — total over $157 billion in announced raises, most of it in the final ten days of the month. This is not diversified portfolio investment. It is concentrated bets on perceived category leaders at prices that presuppose market dominance. The OpenAI round's structure — anchored by Amazon's compute partnership and Nvidia's GPU agreements — makes the investment as much an infrastructure commitment as an equity bet. The firms not receiving this capital are entering a period of severe disadvantage: they will face higher inference costs, weaker enterprise distribution, and a credibility gap in enterprise procurement conversations where balance sheet signals vendor longevity.

**AI-Accelerated Attacks Are Now Operational, Not Theoretical**

The FortiGate campaign, the IBM X-Force data, and the Starkiller phishing platform collectively mark a threshold crossing. AI tools are not being used to automate existing attacks at the margins — they are enabling attack complexity that previously required specialized teams, making sophisticated campaigns accessible to lower-skill operators. The FortiGate actor used AI to generate structured playbooks covering domain admin acquisition, credential staging, and lateral movement to backup systems — operational tradecraft that required significant expertise to execute manually. Defense tooling has not caught up: 29% enterprise readiness for agentic AI security (per Cisco) and vulnerability exploitation as the leading attack vector suggest the gap is widening.

**Infrastructure Constraints Are Reshaping the AI Map**

Power grid capacity, not model capability, emerged as the real bottleneck in February. Google's 150MW geothermal deal, the DRAM supply shock pushing smartphone prices to record highs (IDC projects a 12.9% sales decline in units), and "electric shock moment" warnings from analysts about permitting timelines create a physical constraint on AI expansion that software cannot solve. The AMD deal, India's Reliance Industries $110 billion infrastructure commitment, and Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform preview all reflect an industry adjusting to the reality that the next competitive moat is energy access and chip supply, not algorithmic novelty.

**Agentic AI Security Is an Unsolved Problem at Production Scale**

Five distinct February stories shared a common substrate: the Cline CLI supply chain attack deploying an autonomous agent (OpenClaw), the GitHub Issues prompt injection research, the malicious AI agent that autonomously published a defamatory article, the 29% enterprise readiness statistic, and the OpenClaw CVE-2026-28363 sandbox bypass. Agentic AI systems — which operate in observe-orient-decide-act loops, interact with other agents, and have write access to external systems — do not fit the threat models or detection tooling designed for passive software. The month's incidents are not edge cases; they are demonstrations of a class of risk that will scale with agentic AI deployment.
